Professional Ethics in Mental Health.

What is Mental Health?

A simple definition of mental health is a person’s condition
with regard to their psychological and emotional well-being.
Mental health has an effect on each and every one of us.
It determines how we as individuals interact with one another,
how we handle stressors in our lives, how we handle
our own thoughts and emotions.

Ethics Involvement in Mental Health.

Confidentiality and privacy in therapy. When it comes to being in therapy,
patients have the right to feel heard, express their thoughts and
feelings without feeling judged. They also have the right to know that whatever information
is being shared will not be repeated outside of the therapist.
Specifically, for children, it becomes a different story in different ways.
Legally, a minor does not have the right to confidentiality in therapy from their parents.
Although, children have the right to privacy from third parties, but not from their parents.

The ethical complications with confidentiality with children are concerns with the circumstances
under which a therapist would disclose information that was shared by the child to their parents.
A therapist is legally obligated to report information shared by a child if he or she believes that
the child’s life is in danger. They are only allowed to share information that will be necessary to protect the child.
While there are laws with confidentiality, psychologists will only disclose information shared by the child to their
parents if it’s to protect the patient from harm, or to provide professional services. Patients that are underage should still
feel safe about disclosing information to their therapists and not have to worry about their parents knowing what was shared.
With this, therapists should inform them that any information that they shared would not always be kept from their parents,
but they could guide the parents or let them decide whether or not they want to know everything that had been discussed.
